CONTEXT · BID LIFECYCLE
Just enough to understand the problem.

TReDS is a regulated invoice discounting auction platform. Suppliers upload invoices. Financiers bid on them. Lowest approved bid wins.

Financiers operate through a mandatory maker-checker workflow. A Maker places the bid. A Checker reviews and approves it. Until the Checker approves — the bid is a draft. It is not live. It does not influence the auction.

This detail — the maker-checker approval gap — is where everything in this case study lives.

Introduction

Not a system failure. Not user error.

It was neither. Which meant it was a design problem. Then they called me in.

The brief was direct: dig deep, find the exact problem, solve it fast. I came in expecting to find a missing notification. What I found was something more fundamental.

The platform had no language for competitive position at all.

Investigation

Five failures. No single villain.

My first instinct — like everyone else's — was a notification problem. Add an alert, close the ticket.

I was wrong. And I am glad I didn't stop there.

When I mapped the full bid lifecycle with the staff engineer — every tab, every state, every transition for Maker and Checker — what emerged was not one problem but five compounding failures that had been invisible because nobody had mapped the system end to end before.

Failure 01
Column showed a value — not ownership
Failure 02
No notification when a counter-bid was placed
Failure 03
Bid status invisible during Checker Pending
Failure 04
Checker approved without context
Failure 05
State transitions happened silently across every tab
Failure 01

The column showed a value. Not ownership.

The Lowest Bid column told a financier what the lowest bid was. It never told them whether that bid was theirs or someone else's.

When both values are identical — as they were in this incident — the column is technically accurate and completely misleading at the same time.

The system was answering the wrong question. Financiers were asking "Am I winning?" The column was answering "What is the lowest rate?"

Failure 02

No notification when a counter-bid was placed.

When another financier entered the same auction, the affected party received nothing. No email. No in-app alert.

The competitive landscape changed in real time. The platform said nothing.

A financier managing 20–40 FUs simultaneously had no way to know one of them had just become contested — while they were watching another screen entirely.

Failure 03

Bid status invisible during Checker Pending.

When a Maker places a bid it moves to Checker Pending. It is not live. The auction continues. Other financiers are bidding.

But the UI gave no indication the Maker's bid was inactive. The Maker saw their bid rate and assumed it was competing. It wasn't.

This was the exact window where the ₹8.97 Cr incident happened — two bids sitting in separate Checker Pending queues, neither party knowing the other existed.

Failure 04

The Checker approved without context.

When a revised bid arrived in the Checker's Pending Review tab, the Checker saw one thing: a bid rate. A single number.

No indication it was a revision. No visibility into the previously approved bid still live on the platform. No competitive position indicator.

They were making a financial approval with a fraction of the information they needed.

Failure 05

State transitions happened silently.

FUs moved between tabs with no explanation of why, what bid was honored, or what happened to pending actions.

The platform treated every transition as a technical event. The user experienced it as a disappearance.

On a financial platform, every time something disappears without explanation — a bid, an FU, a pending action — the user fills the silence with their worst assumption.

The Pushbacks

Not everything was immediately accepted.

Two pushbacks shaped the final design significantly. Both forced a better solution than I would have built without the resistance.

Pushback 01
"Just add a notification — that's all we need."
A notification would solve Failure 2. It would leave Failures 1, 3, 4, and 5 untouched. A financier could receive the alert, check the platform, see My Bid 5.5% and Lowest Bid 5.5%, and still not know they were losing.
Pushback 02
"We can't show competitor information."
The position indicator requires zero competitor data. The session knows the financier's own approved bids. The platform knows the lowest bid. The comparison is a simple binary — is this mine or not? No competitor name. No competitor rate. The constraint was real. The solution worked within it completely.
The Edge Case

While mapping the revision flow — I caught something nobody had.

If a buyer closes an auction while a revised bid is sitting in Checker Pending — the system correctly honors the last confirmed approved bid, not the draft revision.

But the Bid Lost tab was showing the draft value — not the confirmed bid that was actually honored.

A financier could bid 5.5%, revise to 5.4%, lose the auction at 5.5%, and see 5.4% on their Bid Lost screen — a bid the platform never treated as real.

I flagged it as P0 critical. It was not in the original brief. It would have shipped undetected.

The draft bid edge case
Step 1 — Approved bid
Financier places and checker approves bid at 5.5% — now live on platform.
Step 2 — Revision in Checker Pending
Maker revises to 5.4%. Awaiting Checker approval. Still a draft.
Step 3 — Auction closes during pending
Buyer closes auction. 5.5% is honored (correct). But Bid Lost tab shows 5.4% — the draft that was never real.
A trust and audit problem. In a regulated financial platform, this is not a minor UX gap.
Solution · Layer 1

Bid Position Indicator.

The Lowest Bid column was redesigned from a value display into a competitive position signal. Zero competitor data required.

The system compares the financier's own approved bid against the platform's lowest bid and returns one of four states. For equal-bid situations — the exact state that caused ₹8.97 Cr in losses — the tiebreaker rule surfaces inline at the exact moment it matters.

Backend requirement: one new output — a four-state position flag per FU per session. LEADING, MATCHED_FIRST, MATCHED_NOT_FIRST, TRAILING. No competitor data. The data to compute it already existed.

Four states — no competitor data exposed
★

You are leading. No other approved bids exist on this FU.
↑

Equal bid exists. You are leading based on earlier approval time.

Trailing

"Equal bids are awarded to the earliest approved bid."
Shown inline on equal-bid states — not in a tooltip, not in a help article.
Solution · Layer 2

Counter-bid Notification.

When a competitor's Checker approves a bid on an FU where the financier has an active approved bid — a notification fires to both the Maker and the Checker.

The trigger is deliberate: not on Maker placement (that's a draft), but on Checker approval — the moment a bid becomes real.

I chose not to notify on routine transitions. When everything is urgent, nothing is urgent. The counter-bid notification needs to land with weight.

Solution · Layer 3

Real-time Attention Hierarchy.

The Lowest Bid column already updated in real time. The problem was that the update happened so fast — a silent number swap in a cell — that a Maker scanning other rows would never register it.

Real-time data that updates silently is not a feature. It is noise.

I designed a four-level attention cascade. A Maker can be looking at any level of the interface — cell, row, page, tab — and the signal will find them.

Four-level cascade — signal finds the user at any zoom
1
Cell — Flash animation
The cell flashes amber when the Lowest Bid value changes. Immediate, local signal.
2
Row — Persistent state
If position deteriorates, the row holds a persistent visual state. It does not silently return to normal.
3
Pagination — Page flag
Pages where competitive events occurred on FUs the Maker isn't viewing are flagged.
4
Tab — Counter badge
The tab itself carries a count of how many FUs need attention, visible from anywhere in the session.
The Rule

I stopped designing individual fixes.

Midway through the tab-by-tab solution, I stopped. The draft edge case, the silent Checker Pending window, the invisible FU disappearance — these were not separate problems. They were symptoms of the same underlying failure.

The platform had no consistent rule for how to communicate state changes. Every time an FU moved between tabs it simply disappeared. No explanation. No confirmation. No record of what had been honored.

I stopped designing individual fixes and proposed a platform-wide design rule instead. Expanding scope mid-sprint. A harder conversation. The right one.

Platform UX Standard — Established post-incident
"Every time an FU moves between tabs or disappears from a tab, the system must never let it transition silently."
Why did this move? — Every transition must be explained.
What was honored? — The confirmed bid always made explicit.
What happened to pending actions? — No action disappears without resolution.
Solution · Checker Flow

The Checker went from approving a number to making a decision.

Mapping the Checker experience separately — not assuming it mirrored the Maker — revealed its own set of failures.

The backend already held both bids as separate records. This was not a data problem. It was a visibility problem. The data existed. Nobody had thought to surface it to the Checker.

I also redesigned the rejection flow. When a Checker rejected a revision, the original approved bid remained live — but the Checker didn't know that. The redesigned rejection confirmation makes the invisible safety net visible.

Principles

Six rules that came out of this.

These didn't exist as a set before the incident. They do now — and they apply to every future decision on this platform.

01
Position over value. A number without context is not information.
02
Notification restraint. When everything is urgent, nothing is urgent.
03
Invisible safety nets create anxiety, not confidence.
04
Compliance forces cleverness. The constraint made the design better.
05
Attention must be earned. Real-time updates that happen silently are noise.
06
Silent transitions are where trust breaks down.

One email. One question. One column.

The column showed what the lowest bid was. It never showed whose it was. That gap — between a number and its meaning — produced a ₹8.97 Cr loss, a suspended operation, and a leadership escalation that should never have had to happen.

What we built was not a notification feature. It was a new standard for how the platform communicates with the people who trust it with their money — across every state a bid can be in, for every person involved in placing and approving it.
